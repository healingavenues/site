    •   The past three decades have seen a dramatic increase of interest in natural health care. Numerous scientific studies have clearly demonstrated that what we eat, drink, and breathe combined with our activities and the quality of the environment play a crucial role in both our short term and long term health. In addition, extensive research verifies that mental and emotional states are both affected by the aforementioned factors while also influencing the lifestyle choices we make, be they healthy or not.

      In the late 1970s, I moved to and lived in a small town on Oahu, part of the Hawaiian chain of islands. This move was a deliberate exercise to explore what Australians refer to as a 'walkabout' i. e. an opportunity to do some soul-searching about the next stage of my life. I recall many hours walking a quiet ocean beach while posing the question "If I could do anything I would like to do, what would that be?", and threw that request out over the ocean, hoping for an insight. After eight or nine months, the answer materialized during an unexpected situation. In a nearby town, while waiting for a natural food store to open, I witnessed a mother, roughly my age, give her beautiful, free-spirited, three-year-old daughter a can of Pepsi and a package of cheesies at 8:55 in the morning! Her mother looked neither happy nor particularly healthy and I had a sinking feeling imagining the health of this child in another twenty-five years. That instance was a life-altering event and I decided in that moment I wanted to do something to reverse that troubling trend in our society!

      Two months later, I came back to Canada. Within a short time of arriving back home in Toronto, I learned about a plan to establish the first full-time program in Canadian history to graduate naturopathic doctors. Along with a group of similarly excited students, I returned to University in order to obtain the necessary prerequisites in healthcare sciences needed to enter what at that time was named The Ontario College of Naturopathic Medicine. That historic effort eventually yielded eight graduates from the O.C.N.M. (now the Canadian College of Naturopathic Medicine), four men and four women naturophatic doctors in 1987. Since that date, there have been some very encouraging developments. These days, patients are growing tired of waiting up to an hour and beyond their scheduled appointment times then sitting passively during a short visit with their medical doctor and obediently following the doctor's orders, often delivered with a paternalistic attitude. Currently, patients are asking more questions, wanting longer visits, sometimes challenging their practitioners, and demanding to be treated as equals. All of this bodes well for a truly cost-effective, thorough and preventitive-oriented healthcare system rather than our current pharmaceutical company dominated disease management model.
